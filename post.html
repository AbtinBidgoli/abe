Krystal Ball & Saagar Enjeti’s Breaking Points is a lot like what TV news would look like if the top dozen or so noxious propaganda imperatives were removed from the broadcast equation. They do a great job of focusing on underlying issues ignored by the usual suspects.

This section would be a compilation of all the information & resources needed to fully implement our proprietary LEAD program. Whether you are a Committee member looking for facilitator notes, a collegiate peer with a specific question about a specific session, a guest facilitator looking for session materials, or just looking to become more involve, this is a great place to start.

An online resource library of supplemental & resource material for clients to learn more about each session's topic ~ before or after the session ~ will soon be available. Phase I, II, III, & IV Client content is prototyping.


Having said that, last week’s headline above a New York Times column by Ezra Klein read:

What if the Unvaccinated Can’t Be Persuaded?

The lede:

I hate that I believe the sentence I’m about to write. It undermines much of what I spend my life trying to do. But there is nothing more overrated in politics ~ perhaps in life ~ than the power of persuasion.
It is nearly impossible to convince people of what they don’t want to believe.

The column followed a predictable path: tales of the unvaccinated writhing on deathbeds as they repented too late, reminders about the positive history of vaccine mandates, & a somber conclusion:

“I urge those who object to vaccination passports as an unprecedented stricture on liberty to widen their tragic imagination.”

Even though I ostensibly persuade people for a living, I no longer believe persuasion works. Here, have some authoritarian solutions was the basic gist. Whatever happened to Yes We Can? “Nothing to fear but fear itself”? “When the going gets weird, the weird turn pro?” So much for the American’s celebrated can-do spirit.
